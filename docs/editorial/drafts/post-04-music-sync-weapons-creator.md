## Tim's raw writing (verbatim)

> POST 4 (Wow, look at that, maybe the music sync does belong at post 4. Because setting up AI use/tool making and Music origins, and me, should come first.)
>
> After splice discovery, the memory is hazy but I think this was one of the first tools that I made (at least, the first I made that still gets used. A lot of junk preceded it). I gotta say, everytime I open this screen I think of how awesome it is that I get to sit and blend my favorite genre of music with scifi gadgets. The process itself is, to me, exactly what it should be. I click through my current selection of organized, unused loops, find one with potential or strikes the right emotion, and then all the visuals and stats and visuals are based on the music, not the other way around. I sit and listen as I click from the waveform/music weapon builder screen, over to the projectiles or beams, and let pure vibes guide the process. Initially if I heard a longer sustained note I would think "Well obviously this needs to be a beam" but since then I've found some of my favorite weapons or short beam bursts.
>
> That visual design process, and the preview window you see, will be covered In a liater post about the insane, fist-pounding-on-desk frustration I had getting preview/audition windows to match what was in game. Hard lessons learned there, but that's another story.
>
> You might expect me to say that the first iteration of the waveform trigger screen was buggy but honestly, I was shocked by how well it worked. It wasn't buggy, but it was of course feature-poor, because I didn't know what I would need. Things like sliders that allow some music loops being faded in and up, in case they have those long notes that don't sound good jumping into the action as soon as the player pulls the trigger.
>
> I will say one thing that took some iteration, and that claude continues to be inconsistent with across all tool creations, is the saving mechanism. For a while there was no way to rename weapons, only save new ones. It was one of those things that was low priority to fix and in that over-excited run and gun arms dealing, I didn't care enough to write the prompt. To this day, some dev tools auto save their changes, others require a click. There was a stretch there where in the level backdrop creator, claude had me clicking to print parameters to debug and paste it into the chat so it could make the changes itself. I cannot explain why I went along with that for as long as I did, but I'm happy to say that's dead now.
>
> As for the music screen though, yeah. Waveform generating was a claude one-shot, and it assumed I needed the triggers to snap-to beat markers. Little quality of life things came later: a ruler on the top that I could click to move the play cursor if I wanted to hear/see a specific section of the track. Buttons that autopopulated rapid fire triggers because sometimes its easier to take triggers away than place them all. Hilariously, for about a month of development I was placing each one by hand. Thinking of it as a sign that this was a labor of love, but really it was a sign of me being stupid.
>
> I've discovered a super weird, ironic kind of laziness during this process. It's being too lazy to prompt claude to build the thing that would make life way easier. Maybe that's not laziness, maybe it's just prioticing progress over improving your own quality of life? Like rooting through a bag of freshly cleaned clothes, trying to find what you want to wear. Putting them away in their drawers would make finding them SO much easier, but making things easier requires time and effort. I'm going off the rails here.
