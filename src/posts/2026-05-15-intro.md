I'm Tim. I'm building a game called SYNTHERION — a top-down vertical shooter where every weapon is bound to an audio loop. Equip a loadout, you've composed a track. Fire in combat, you're mixing it.

This site is where I'm going to talk about how it's actually going.

## Who's writing this

I started out wanting to make movies. That became wanting to write. That became an engineering degree I'm in the middle of. Somewhere in there I built bots, tried board games, ran a calculus YouTube channel — and none of it hit. I still ghostwrite to pay rent.

It turns out the two skills I've been bouncing between for fifteen years — telling stories and designing systems — are exactly the two skills you need to build a game like this. I don't know if that's a happy accident or the kind of thing you only see clearly looking back.

## The honest version of how this gets built

I'm working with Claude. Specifically, [Claude Code](https://claude.com/claude-code). I tell it what the game is supposed to be — what it should *feel* like, the vocabulary, the architecture — and most of the actual code gets written by it. The most important file in the repo isn't a script; it's a design briefing called `CLAUDE.md` that I keep teaching about itself.

This isn't "AI made my game." At time of writing I'm somewhere north of 250 hours into the project, and most of those hours were spent figuring out what the game wants to be — then iterating, course-correcting, and explaining when something Claude built was wrong in ways that took me a day to articulate. The leverage isn't "AI writes faster." It's that I can stay in the part of the work I'm good at (deciding what's right) and lean on a partner that's better than me at the part I'm not (typing it correctly the first time).

I'll write more about how this actually works in the next few posts.

## What's planned for this site

I have four months of material I haven't talked about yet, including a *very* different earlier version of this game where players were supposed to make their own music (it was bad — but the screenshots are funny). I'm going to dig that up.

Most posts will be about the in-game dev tools I've built. They are, almost without exception, ugly. They were never meant to be shown. That's a feature for the dev log — there's something honest about a tool that exists to do exactly one job and looks every bit of it.

If you want to follow along, the two things that matter:

- The **Discord** is where the conversation actually happens. I want player input to land, and that's where it lands.
- The **mailing list** is for people who'd rather not check in but want to know when there's a release.

Both are in the bar at the bottom of every page.
